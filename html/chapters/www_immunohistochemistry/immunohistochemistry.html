1. Introduction

Immunohistochemistry in C. elegans includes two major classes of techniques: immunocytochemical localization of proteins in situ and Western blot analysis of purified proteins. In this section, we will discuss a variety of techniques for immunocytochemistry, as well as simple Western blotting techniques for worm proteins.

Immunocytochemistry provides the most direct method for identifying both the cellular and subcellular distribution of your protein. Although GFP or other translational or transcriptional constructs can provide a relatively rapid indication of gene expression or protein distribution, there are a number of potential problems with these techniques (Mello and Fire, 1995). These include abnormal regulation of expression from multi-copy transgenic arrays and abnormal localization of the protein due to over-expression or the presence of the tag. If possible, the predicted distribution of your protein should be confirmed with the use of antibodies in situ. Specific antibodies also allow you to examine protein modifications and expression in vivo.

Although antibodies can be very useful tools, generating a specific antibody protein can be difficult, time-consuming, and expensive (see Harlow and Lane, 1988). Unfortunately, except for antibodies to a few highly conserved proteins (e.g., actin and tubulin), most antibodies generated against specific vertebrate or invertebrate proteins do not cross-react specifically with their C. elegans homologs. Furthermore, when you specifically generate an antibody against a C. elegans protein, the antibody may only work with a subset of immune techniques, e.g., Westerns but not in situ or vice versa. A brief Antigen preparation protocol highlights some considerations when designing antigens.

Whenever you use antibodies, it is important to test for specificity. In addition to the standard controls, such as pre-absorbance of your antibody, worm-specific methods may be available to test antibody specificity. If there are null mutants or RNAi worms for the gene and protein of interest, then compare antibody staining (of Westerns or in situ) in those strains with staining in wild-type worms. Nulls can also be used to affinity deplete your serum (see Depletion of primary antibody protocol). On the other hand, if there are transgenic over-expressing lines for the protein of interest, those can be used as positive controls. Generally, you will need to do some sort of affinity depletion before you can detect specific staining of Westerns or worms (see Affinity purification protocol).

1.1. Immunocytochemistry

C. elegans provides specific rewards and particular challenges for in situ analysis of protein distribution. If you have an antibody that works in situ, you can examine the distribution of the protein in the entire organism at all stages relatively rapidly. Other than the intestine, most tissues have relatively low levels of autofluorescence, so many different dyes and secondary antibodies may be used, allowing triple labeling of antibodies or more.

On the other hand, immunocytochemistry in worms is relatively difficult due to problems of access and size. Both the egg shell of embryos and the cuticles of larvae and adults are relatively impermeable and must be disrupted chemically or mechanically (or both) for the antibody to have access to the internal cells and proteins. The small size of the individual cells and their compact architecture can make visualization of the cellular and subcellular distribution of proteins in some cells quite difficult. A confocal fluorescent microscope or a wide-field fluorescent microscope with de-convolution software can provide increased spatial resolution. When detailed examination of subcellular distribution is necessary, then one can attempt the supreme challenge of immuno-electron microscopy. For a discussion of electron microscopy and immuno-EM in C. elegans (not covered in this section), see the Worm Atlas website.

There are several different techniques used to allow antibodies access to the tissue of interest. If you are specifically interested in the distribution of a protein within the cells of the gonad, the very early embryo, or the intestine, then the appropriate tissue can be dissected out of the adult (see Freeze-crack protocol). To examine protein in a larva or adult, the two general methods are to 1) collect worms in small tubes of liquid and to freeze and chemically treat them to disrupt their cuticles (see Tube fixation, Bouin's tube fixation, Peroxide tube fixation, and Picric acid + glutaraldehyde fixation protocols) or 2) compress and freeze worms between slides and mechanically pull off the cuticle layer (see Freeze-crack protocol). Different fixation conditions differentially preserve antigenicity and tissue morphology. The Fixation conditions protocol discusses how to determine the optimum fixation conditions for a particular antibody and protein combination. You can use a control antibody such as anti-DNA or anti-actin to monitor accessibility and morphology with the different fixation conditions.

1.2. Western blot analysis

Western blot analysis is useful for several reasons. It can be used to examine the relative levels of expression of different size forms of your protein, such as those due to alternative splicing. It can help identify post-translational modifications such as phosphorylation or glycosylation. Western blot analysis can be used to screen for changes in protein expression under different conditions or in different mutant backgrounds. Changes in protein expression may also be seen with immunocytochemistry (and examined in a cell-specific way). But, generally antibodies do not distinguish between protein variants in situ unless specifically designed to do so. And some antibody works well for Westerns but not in situ.

Preparation of denatured protein from C. elegans is relatively straight forward, since simple boiling of adults or embryos in a typical protein buffer frees many proteins from the body (see Worm protein preparation protocol). A major difficulty with Western blot analysis arises from the fact that the entire worm is usually used for protein preparation. Thus, even if your protein is of fair abundance in a particular tissue, e.g., the nervous system, its abundance relative to total worm protein may be quite low. Therefore, sensitive detection methods are generally required (see Western transfer protocol).

If you generated your antibody using a fusion protein or a coupled peptide, then first test your antibody for staining of a Western Blot using a very low concentration of this protein. If you can not detect the protein in this purified condition, you are unlikely to be able to detect it in a complex mixture of worm proteins. The antibody may still work on worms in situ, since the antigen will be in a different conformation in the fixed worm than in the protein gel.

When you test an antibody on a worm preparation, it is helpful if you can use a Western blot from a tissue or strain that is enriched for the protein. For example, if the protein is enriched in a particular stage, then preparation of synchronous populations is recommended. If a particular enriched tissue can be dissected out fairly rapidly (e.g., the gonad), that is also recommended as the starting preparation. If you have a transgenic strain or mutant that over-expresses your protein, then use it to test for specificity of antibody binding in Westerns.

1.3. General comments

C. elegans cell culture (Christensen et al., 2002) provides novel tools for immunohistochemistry. Cell culture provides an easy method to test antibodies for staining of cells without concerns about permeability and accessibility (see Fixation conditions protocol). It also may provide an enriched source of particular cell types (e.g., sorted GFP-positive cells) for use in Western analysis. Despite this, the intact organism continues as the major focus of immunohistochemical techniques, as described in the following protocols.

2. Protocols and procedures

2.1. Protocol 1: Antigen preparation

2.1.1. General comments

For a thorough discussion of antigens and antibody generation, see Harlow and Lane (1988).

2.1.1.1. Antibody production

There are a number of companies that will assist you in your antibody production, from the generation of fusion proteins or peptide antigens through serum purification. See the following for suggestions for peptide design and coupling (see Peptide coupling protocol) and affinity purification (see Antibody purification protocol).

2.1.1.2. Animal host

The most frequently immunized animal is the rabbit. If you plan to do any double labeling, try generating your antibodies in one or two different species. Chickens are recommended: they are relatively inexpensive, the collection of antibody in eggs is humane, and they often generate robust immune responses. It is generally more economical to have the company deliver the eggs to you rather than the purified serum. Chicken antibodies are easy and inexpensive to partially purify from the egg yolks (see Chicken antibody purification protocol).

2.1.1.3. Fusion proteins

Generation of fusion proteins for immunization is much more laborious than buying peptides, but immunization with fusion proteins is more likely to lead to useful antibodies. If you have a company generate the fusion protein for you, be certain to request sufficient protein for testing, affinity purification, and affinity depletion of your serum.

Green Fluorescent Protein can be a wonderful protein to use in translational fusions (see Reporter gene fusions section of WormMethods). If expression of a translational GFP fusion leads to rescue of the mutant phenotype, then the location of GFP fluorescence in the rescued animals is likely to correspond at least partially to the normal localization of your protein. Of course, the localization should be checked with immunocytochemical localization of the endogenous protein in a wild-type animal. If your protein is not functional or stable in a GFP fusion protein, there are a number of very small epitope tags such as 6-His and Myc that can be fused to your protein and used for preliminary localization studies (Roehl et al., 1996).

2.1.1.4. Peptides

Immunization with synthetic peptides is the quickest and easiest way to try and generate antibodies, although it is not always productive. Companies will predict immunogenic peptides or you can design the peptides yourself (http://immunax.dfci.harvard.edu/Tools/antigenic.html). If they are potentially immunogenic, then N-terminal or C-terminal peptides are recommended.

Be sure to order enough peptide for testing, affinity purification, and affinity depletion of your serum.

Use at least two different peptides from the same protein: peptides are inexpensive and many peptides are not immunogenic or are not exposed on the fixed protein. If cost is a major concern, two peptides from the same protein can be injected together during immunization; the serum can be specifically affinity purified as needed.

Peptides are generally conjugated to carrier proteins such as KLH (keyhole limpet hemocyanin), most easily using a kit and a peptide with an extra N-terminal cysteine. Peptides may also be coupled to serum albumin (see Peptide coupling protocol).

Peptides can be conjugated to two different carrier proteins (e.g., KLH and serum albumin). One conjugate can be used for immunization and the other conjugate can be used for affinity purification (see Antibody purification protocol).

2.2. Protocol 2: Peptide coupling

2.2.1. General comments

This is a very simple and rapid procedure that uses glutaraldehyde to cross-link a peptide to serum albumin as the carrier protein (Harlow and Lane, 1988). The serum albumin should be from the species that will be immunized, e.g., if you plan to immunize a chicken you should use chicken serum albumin as the carrier protein. The basic premise is that the immunized animal will not generate many antibodies against its own serum albumin.

2.2.2. Protocol

  1. Dissolve 5 mg peptide in 1 ml sterile 100 mM NaPO4 buffer, pH 7 in a 1.5 ml tube.

  2. Dissolve 20 mg serum albumin in 1 ml sterile 100 mM NaPO4 buffer in a separate 1.5 ml tube.

  3. Mix peptide and albumin solutions in a tiny clear beaker and stir well with stir bar.

  4. In hood: Stir constantly while adding drops of 20 mM glutaraldehyde (TOXIC). Add the drops at a rate of 100 ul per 5 minutes until up to 1 ml of glutaraldehyde has been added over a period of almost one hour.

    Ideally, the solution should be slightly yellow when the protein is slightly cross-linked. If the solution turns yellow before the full 1 ml of glutaraldehyde has been added, stop adding the glutaraldehyde.

  5. Dialyze at 4°C with PBS. Do at least 3 changes each with one liter of PBS for several hours.

  6. Divide into 10 or 20 1.5 ml tubes and speedvac (no heat) until dry. Assume each tube has approximately 1 or 2 mg of protein-coupled peptide.

  7. Store at -20°C in well-sealed aliquots.

2.3. Protocol 3: Chicken antibody purification

2.3.1. General comments

This antibody enrichment procedure is based on the protocol of Gassmann et al. (1990). It uses serial PEG precipitations to enrich for the antibodies. The end product, although far from pure, is stable and is suitable for immunocytochemistry or Western blotting. Further purification is needed before immunoprecipitation.

2.3.2. Protocol

  1. Carefully break egg into palm of gloved hand or into an egg separator.

  2. Using a squirt bottle of cold dd water, rinse the yolk.

  3. Remove and discard as much of the white as possible.

  4. Drop cleaned yolk into a beaker with a stir bar.

  5. Break yolk with a pipette, if necessary.

  6. Add cold buffer A, ~ 10 ml per yolk.

  7. Stir on stir plate for >2 minutes.

  8. Stir in 30 ml per yolk of cold 7% PEG.

  9. When well mixed (>5 min), pour into centrifuge bottle (leave behind the scum on beaker).

  10. Centrifuge at 14,000 g for 10 minutes.

  11. Pour the top two layers (a thin layer of oil and a liquid layer) through a funnel with a Whatman #1 filter into a new centrifuge bottle. Discard the yellow pellet.

  12. Add PEG 8,000 powder to a final concentration of 12g/100ml fluid.

  13. Shake in centrifuge bottle on shaker at 4°C for 1-2 hours, until the PEG is in solution.

    Note

    Do not use a stir bar and beaker since the antibody will stick to the stir bar and beaker.

  14. Centrifuge at 14,000 g for 10 minutes.

  15. Pour off and discard fluid, leaving behind orange pellet.

  16. Spin pellet at 5,000 rpm for 2 minutes.

  17. Remove with any fluid with a pipette and discard.

  18. Add 5 ml per yolk of buffer A.

  19. Dissolve the pellet in the buffer by stirring it with a glass rod (laborious) or by letting it sit or shake overnight at 4°C.

  20. If desired (not necessary for antibody for staining), remove residual PEG with dialysis or ammonium sulfate precipitation.

2.3.3. Comments

For maximum yield, have all solutions and do all work at 4°C.

Obtain and process pre-immune eggs. Pre-immune chickens may have anti-nematode antibodies.

Start by processing a single egg per beaker. Once a plateau of antibody production has been reached (after 3 immunizations), you may process together all the eggs produced in one week by one hen.

Intact eggs will last at least 3 weeks at 4°C.

Note

IgY, the antibody in yolks, does NOT bind to Protein A columns.

2.4. Protocol 4: Affinity purification

2.4.1. General comments

Pre-absorbing antibody with a bacterial acetone powder may improve the specificity of the binding of your antibody, especially if your antibody was generated against a bacterially expressed fusion protein (Harlow and Lane, 1988; Miller and Shakes, 1995). However, affinity purification has the potential for a much more significant improvement in specificity.

This protocol uses nitrocellulose membranes rather than columns for antibody purification (Smith and Fisher, 1984; Duerr et al., 1999). This technique minimizes the time that the antibodies spend in the elution buffer in order to maximize antibody recovery. It is also quick, simple, and inexpensive.

If you have used a KLH-coupled peptide as your antigen, you can affinity purify your antibody with a serum albumin-coupled peptide to increase specificity (or vice versa).

If you do not have a pure antigen protein preparation, you may still use this method to purify your antibody. Run your protein mixture (e.g., partially purified fusion protein) out on a gel and then Western blot it to a high-quality, high-binding nitrocellulose membrane. Cut out the area of the membrane containing the blotted protein of interest and use the membrane in the following procedure.

This protocol can also be used to affinity deplete your serum. For example, if you have used a partially purified fusion protein as your antigen, you can use a partially purified preparation for a different fusion protein to bind (and discard) antibodies that recognize the non-specific shared portion of the fusion protein or the bacterial contaminants.

2.4.2. Protocol

Procedure 1. Coating of membrane with antigen

  1. Add 10 ml NFDM to a 100 mm petri dish (to block any protein binding sites on the dish).

  2. Shake at low speed (40–60 rpm) for 1 hour at room temperature (or longer at 4°C).

  3. Rinse dish 3X with TBST (about 20 ml per wash) and drain well.

  4. Add 15 ml Antibody Transfer Solution.

    [Optional: save sample to check protein concentration in Antibody Transfer Solution.]

  5. Place 1 nitrocellulose membrane into dish with forceps (not fingers).

  6. Shake 30-60 minutes at room temperature (or longer at 4°C).

    [Save unbound Antibody Transfer Solution and check for protein concentration.]

  7. Rinse membrane in dish 3X with TBST.

  8. Cover membrane with NFDM.

  9. Put on shaker for 1 hour at room temperature (or longer at 4°C).

    Note

    Antigen-coated membrane may be stored in a dish in a small volume of NFDM at -20°C.

Procedure 2. Pre-elution of membrane

  1. Rinse membrane in the coated dish 3X with TBST.

  2. Rinse briefly with dd water.

  3. Add 10 ml glycine buffer.

  4. Swirl for exactly 60 seconds.

  5. Pour off and discard glycine buffer.

  6. Quickly rinse membrane 3X with TBST.

  7. In hood, rinse briefly with dd water.

  8. Add 10 ml TEA buffer (TOXIC).

  9. Swirl for exactly 60 seconds.

  10. Pour off and discard TEA buffer.

  11. Quickly rinse 3X with TBST.

  12. Cover membrane in dish with NFDM.

  13. Shake 30 minutes at room temperature.

Procedure 3. Incubation with antibodies

  1. Rinse membrane in dish 2X with TBST.

  2. Add 10 ml TBST to dish.

  3. Add 0.5 ml (or less) serum to dish.

  4. Shake for 1 hour at room temperature (or longer at 4°C).

    [Save antibody solution after binding (see comments below).]

  5. Rinse membranes 3-6X for 5 minutes each with TBST.

Procedure 4. Elution of antibodies

Important

Do only one membrane at a time to minimize antibody denaturation

  1. Prepare a 15 ml tube with 150 ul 1M Tris pH 8.2 for glycine rinse.

  2. Prepare a 15 ml tube with 800 ul 0.5M Tris pH 6.8 + 200 ul 1M HCl for TEA rinse.

  3. Rinse membrane briefly with dd water.

  4. Add 5 ml glycine buffer.

  5. Swirl for exactly 20 seconds and pour off and save glycine buffer in tube with Tris pH 8.2.

  6. Immediately add another 5 ml glycine buffer to dish with membrane.

  7. Swirl membrane in second rinse of glycine buffer for 20 seconds.

  8. Pour second glycine rinse in with the first rinse.

  9. Quickly rinse membrane 3X with TBST.

  10. In hood, rinse membrane briefly in dd water.

  11. Add 5 ml TEA buffer (TOXIC).

  12. Swirl for exactly 20 seconds and pour off and save TEA buffer in tube with Tris pH 6.8.

  13. Immediately add another 5 ml TEA buffer to dish with membrane.

  14. Swirl membrane in second rinse of TEA buffer for 20 seconds.

  15. Pour second TEA rinse in with the first TEA rinse.

  16. Quickly rinse membrane 3X with TBST.

  17. Check pH of eluates and adjust to pH ~7.5 with appropriate pH Tris BEFORE STORING.

    Since the volumes are so small, use pH strips to check the pH.

  18. Cover used membrane with NFDM and store in dish at -20°C for later re-use.

Procedure 5. Optional concentration

  1. Put glycine eluate in the top of a Centricon or other concentrator (mw cutoff <10-40,000D).

  2. Add plain Phosphate Buffered Saline (PBS) to fill line.

  3. Balance tubes and spin 20-30 minutes at 5,500 rpm at 4°C (or as directed) until concentrated.

  4. Discard flow through.

  5. Add PBS to 15 ml and repeat spin.

  6. Discard flow through.

  7. Add Antibody buffer to 15 ml and repeat spin.

  8. Discard flow through.

  9. Repeat spin if needed until volume <1.5 ml.

  10. Store concentrate in a 1.5 ml capped tube at 4°C or -20°C.

  11. Repeat for TEA eluate.

2.4.3. Comments

Nitrocellulose membranes must be high quality with high protein binding. We use Nitroplus transfer membranes 82 mm discs (MSI).

Never touch the membranes with your fingers; always transfer them with forceps.

Antigen-coated membranes may be used more than once. Store them in NFDM at -20°C. Membranes will show decreased purification effectiveness after 2-10 uses, depending upon the antigen and serum.

Be sure to have the Tris solutions ready to neutralize the buffers immediately after the elution steps. Elution and neutralization should take about 1 minute per buffer. Adjust pH to 7.5 before storing.

You may do several membranes simultaneously. However, be sure to do the elution step sequentially so that the antibody does not sit in the high or low pH buffer too long.

Glycine elutions have been more productive, but TEA elutions usually provide some specific antibodies in lower concentrations. Note that the glycine elution is a high salt and low pH elution.

The eluates may be stored at 4°C or -20°C; concentrate the antibody before storage.

If you are testing a new antibody, the eluates can be used after pH adjustment but before concentration for Westerns or immunocytochemistry. Try eluate diluted 1:1 - 1:50 in Antibody buffer with 1% BSA (for immunocytochemistry) or Antibody buffer with 1% NFDM (for Westerns).

The optimum amount of serum to use with each round of purification will vary. Start with 0.5 ml serum per membrane, but save the unbound serum and test it with another round of purification.

2.5. Protocol 5: Fixation conditions

2.5.1. General comments

Purify your antibody before testing (see Antibody purification protocol), since non-specific staining of unpurified antibodies usually masks specific staining in C. elegans.

Although a few antibodies work with a wide range of tissue fixation conditions, your antibody is likely to work best (or only) with a particular fixation regime (see Harlow and Lane (1988) and Harlow (1999) for discussion). Major classes of fixatives are precipitating agents (e.g., methanol and acetone) or compounds that cross-link proteins (e.g., formaldehyde and glutaraldehyde).

After fixation, you must permeabilize the tissue to allow access of the antibodies. This is done by the inclusion of a non-ionic detergent in the incubation buffers. After or during particularly hard fixation conditions, treatment with an organic solvent such as methanol or acetone may be necessary for adequate permeabilization (Harlow and Lane, 1988).

In C. elegans, chemical treatments can be used to permeabilize the cuticle, including incubation in collagenase or reduction in β-mercaptoethanol (Finney and Ruvkun, 1990; Garriga et al., 1993; Miller and Shakes, 1995). However, these treatments may also destroy the ability of antibodies to recognize your protein. Therefore, initially test fixation conditions on freeze-cracked or otherwise mechanically disrupted worms or tissues. Or, if applicable, examine dissected tissue (see Freeze-crack protocol; Crittenden et al., 1994) or cultured cells (Christensen et al., 2002). Once the optimum fixation conditions for mechanically disrupted or dissected worms have been determined, then determine whether one of the Tube-fixation protocols that use chemicals to disrupt the cuticle might be more convenient.

You can monitor tissue preservation and accessibility at a gross level by examining the morphology of nuclei stained with the DNA-binding dye DAPI (see Staining slides protocol). DAPI permeates tissue more readily than antibodies; if you do not see DAPI labeling of your cells, then you are unlikely to see antibody staining.

For more specific (but more expensive) examination of tissue accessibility with any fixation method, use a control primary antibody. Good controls are an antibody to DNA (Chemicon mouse monoclonal antibody MAB030) or to a cytoskeletal protein (Amersham L9 anti-tubulin; Calbiochem Ab-1 anti-actin; ICN Biochemicals C4 anti-actin). All of these antibodies work on worms prepared with a variety of fixation conditions.

2.5.2. Protocol

All fixatives are TOXIC.

Try the following with freeze-cracked slides or tissues or cells (See Freeze-crack protocol):

  1. 100% methanol: 2 minutes on ice.

  2. 100% acetone: 2 minutes on ice.

  3. 100% methanol 4 minutes on ice followed by 100% acetone 4 minutes on ice.

  4. 1% formaldehyde in 0.1 M Phosphate buffer + 50% acetone: 30 minutes on ice.

  5. 0.5% formaldehyde in 0.1M Phosphate buffer: 1 hour on ice.

  6. 4% formaldehyde in 0.1M Phosphate buffer: 1 hour on ice.

  7. 4% formaldehyde in 0.1M Phosphate buffer: overnight at 4°C.

  8. 4% formaldehyde + 0.1% glutaraldehyde in 0.1M Phosphate buffer: overnight at 4°C.

  9. 4% formaldehyde + 1% glutaraldehyde in 0.1M Phosphate buffer: 24 hours at 4°C.

    Try the following with tube-fixed worms (See Tube fixation protocol):

  10. 1% formaldehyde: 1 hour on ice.

  11. 4% formaldehyde: overnight at 4°C.

2.5.3. Comments

See the Freeze-crack protocol for recipes for different fixatives. Formaldehyde and glutaraldehyde solutions should be made fresh.

Start with #3 and #6, and then try other conditions as needed. As you discover the optimum conditions, you may change the concentration of fixative, incubation duration, temperature, and the buffer (for example, you can try a buffer including picric acid and/or borate or methanol). Generally, fixation is done in cold solutions to prevent diffusion before fixation.

As you go down the list, the fixation conditions get harder, i.e., more precipitated and/or cross-linked. As this happens, the fixation conditions get more difficult to evaluate because the antibodies will not penetrate the tissue as well, autofluorescence will increase, and fewer worms will stick to the slides. If you need long formaldehyde and/or glutaraldehyde fixation conditions, you can switch from the Freeze-crack to the Tube fixation protocol. The tube fixation procedures are longer and have more variables than the freeze-crack procedure, but the morphology of hard fixed worms is much better with tube fixation.

If you are going to be testing multiple antibodies (or multiple batches of one antibody) you can keep stocks of unstained tube-fixed worms in the refrigerator or -20°C freezer (see Tube fixation protocol and Bouin's tube fixation protocols). Tube-fixed worms last months in the refrigerator or indefinitely at -20°C; although some antigenicity may be lost, fixed worms that are 5+ years old can show specific antibody binding.

If you do cell culture, you can fix and save your old preparations with a variety of fixatives. Store the fixed slides in PBS at 4°C or protected from dehydration at -20°C and use as needed to test antibodies. If you find a protocol that appears to give you specific staining, then use similar fixation conditions on freeze-cracked worms or dissected tissues.

2.6. Protocol 6: Freeze-crack

2.6.1. General comments

Freeze-cracking provides a quick and simple way to remove portions of the worm's cuticle, allowing penetration of antibodies (Strome and Wood, 1982; Albertson, 1984; Miller and Shakes, 1995; Duerr et al., 1999). Dissection of worms (Crittenden et al., 1994) can increase antibody access to some tissues and early embryos.

The procedure generally begins with a large number of worms and ends with a few stained worms. The morphology of the worms is variable; preparation of relatively intact worms takes practice. However, the ease and speed of the technique and the minimal chemical disruption are very useful for evaluating antibodies and are optimum for localizing some antigens.

2.6.2. Protocol

Procedure 6. Freezing plates of worms on slides

Use a plate of healthy worms.

  1. Place large flat slabs of dry ice or place a flat metal plate firmly on top of crushed dry ice in a Styrofoam container.

  2. Wash the worms from the plate with M9 buffer or dd water and collect them in a 1.5 ml tube.

  3. Spin the worms at 2000 rpm (~400 g) for 2-3 minutes.

    [Optional: To enrich for adults, let the worms settle for 5 minutes at room temperature.]

  4. Remove the supernatant and repeat M9 wash until ~ all bacteria are gone and solution is clear.

  5. Rinse worms with dd water, spin or let settle, and remove all but about 50 ul water.

  6. Label lab-made poly-lysine slides with indelible ink and place on counter frosted side up.

    Note

    You can make at least 2-3 slides per plate of worms.

  7. Using a glass pipette, place a small drop (10-20 ul) of worms on the slide.

  8. Let the worms settle briefly.

  9. Use the pipette to spread out the worms and liquid so that individual worms are not overlapping.

  10. Carefully press an unlabeled (commercial) poly-lysine slide down on the worms label side down so that only the unfrosted parts overlap.

  11. Press straight down slightly, enough to immobilize but not rupture most of the adults.

  12. Carefully (without sliding) put the slides on a flat piece of dry ice for 5-30 minutes.

  13. Proceed to Methanol and/or acetone fixation or Formaldehyde and/or glutaraldehyde fixation.

Procedure 7. Freezing individuals or small numbers of worms

Note

A much larger fraction of fixed worms can be recovered with the following procedure.

  1. Transfer the worm(s) to a food-free plate and let them rid themselves of bacteria.

    Note

    If any bacteria get onto the poly-lysine slide, then the worms will not stick as well.

  2. Place a small drop of dd water on the middle of a sticky lab-made poly-lysine slide.

  3. Pick the worms up individually with a clean pick and shake them off in the water drop.

    The worms should partially stick as they fall and touch the poly-lysine.

  4. Multiple (up to ~50) worms of similar sizes can be done on the same slide.

    The more worms you do on a slide, the larger the fraction that you will lose.

  5. When you have transferred the worms, place a commercial poly-lysine slide over the worms.

    For a few worms, the weight of the slide should provide sufficient compression.

    With many worms, press down evenly and lightly to compress the worms.

  6. The water droplet should not reach the edges of the slide after compression.

  7. Immediately and smoothly transfer the slide to a flat piece of dry ice or metal on dry ice.

  8. Proceed to Methanol and/or acetone fixation or Formaldehyde and/or glutaraldehyde fixation.

Procedure 8. Dissecting worms: for intestine, gonad, early embryos

  1. Place a small drop of 0.2 mM levamisole in dd water onto a lab-made poly-lysine slide.

    The levamisole paralyzes and contracts the worms and aids dissection.

  2. Transfer several adults to the drop.

  3. Slice the adults near the head, vulva, or tail with two fresh 25-26 gauge needles.

    Options: Use dissection scissors, or a blade breaker/holder and fine razor blade.

  4. With practice, the intestine and gonad will extrude and stick to the poly-lysine slide.

  5. Early embryos can be pulled free from the rest of the worm.

    Option: Bleach gravid adults, collect embryos and place in a drop of dd water on the slide.

  6. When you have dissected several adults, place a commercial poly-lysine slide over the worms.

    If necessary, press down lightly and evenly on the top slide until it touches the tissues.

    Note

    The water droplet should not reach the edges of the slide after compression.

  7. Immediately and smoothly transfer the slide to a flat piece of dry ice or a metal plate on dry ice.

  8. Proceed to Methanol and/or acetone fixation or Formaldehyde and/or glutaraldehyde fixation.

Procedure 9. Freezing older embryos (from a mixed population)

  1. Pipette M9 over the surface of a plate containing worms in food.

  2. Allow the adults and larvae to thrash free of the food and into the fluid.

    Note

    The immobile embryos will stay within the food.

  3. Tilt plate and pipette off the M9 with thrashing worms and discard.

  4. If adults and larvae are still present on the plate, repeat the M9 wash.

  5. When the plate contains only embryos in food, use a stream of M9 to rinse off the embryos.

  6. Collect embryos into a 1.5 ml tube.

  7. Spin the worms at 2000 rpm (<800 g) for 2-3 minutes.

  8. Replace supernatant with dd water.

  9. Repeat spin and rinse as needed until embryos are clear of bacteria.

  10. Transfer embryos in a small drop of water to a lab-made poly-lysine slide.

  11. Press down lightly and evenly on the top slide until it contacts the embryos.

    Note

    The water droplet should not reach the edges of the slide after compression.

  12. Immediately and smoothly transfer the slide to a flat piece of dry ice or metal plate on dry ice.

  13. Proceed to Methanol and/or acetone fixation or Formaldehyde and/or glutaraldehyde fixation.

Procedure 10. Methanol and/or acetone fixation

See Fixation conditions protocol for variations with organic solvent fixatives.

  1. Place a covered coplin jar of 100% acetone on ice (or in dry ice).

  2. Place a covered coplin jar of 100% methanol on ice (or in dry ice).

  3. Let chill until very cold (>15 minutes).

  4. Crack the slide sandwich by swiftly twisting apart the two slides; discard the top (unlabeled) slide.

    [Optional (for multiple slides): Immediately place the wormless sides of the slides (smooth sides) back-to-back]

  5. Immediately immerse in ice cold methanol for 2+ minutes.

    1. Immerse in ice cold acetone for 4+ minutes.

      [Optional: Filter the acetone and methanol through a Whatman #1 filter and reuse 3-4 times.]

      [Use cold methanol or acetone alone for <10 minutes (see Fixation conditions protocol).]

  7. [Optional: Air-dry the slides for 1-5 minutes before proceeding. This may increase adhesion of the worms to the slide, but may decrease antigenicity.]

  8. Place the slides in a new coplin jar.

  9. Pour PBS down the side of the jar (not directly onto the worms).

  10. Optional: Rehydrate the worms with a series of alcohol solutions (95%, 70%, 50%, and 30%).

  11. Proceed with blocking and staining (see Staining slides protocol).

Procedure 11. Formaldehyde and/or glutaraldehyde fixation: TOXIC

See Fixation conditions protocol and solutions (below) for fixatives.

  1. Prepare FRESH TOXIC formaldehyde and/or glutaraldehyde solution in hood.

  2. Pre-chill fixative on ice.

  3. Crack the slide sandwich by swiftly twisting apart the two slides; discard the top (unlabeled) slide.

  4. Quickly place the slides (worms up) flat in a dish (with lid) in the cold room or refrigerator.

  5. Cover the worms on each slide with 100-200 ul fixative.

  6. Fix the worms for 30 minutes to 24 hours at 4°C.

  7. [Optional: Incubate the slides back to back in a coplin jar containing cold fixative.]

    [Incubation in the jar increases loss of worms from the slide, but is less messy.]

  8. After fixation, rinse the slides gently several times with PBS (until there is no fixative odor).

  9. Proceed with blocking and staining (see Staining slides protocol).

2.6.3. Comments

Use plates containing healthy well-fed worms since starved worms have increased autofluorescence in the gut. Also, dauers seldom crack open.

If you use a mixed population, then most of the worms that stick to the slides will be the fatter adults, since they adhere best to the top and bottom slides. You can use synchronized populations of worms if you wish to recover larvae of a particular age.

Only a fraction of embryos will be cracked open when you use a mixed population. To specifically stain embryos, use a population of embryos that have been transferred to a poly-lysine slide (Procedure 19 or 20).

Clean (bacteria-free) worms will stick the best to the poly-lysine slides. However, bacteria-free worms also stick well to clean plastic or glass pipettes. Use the same glass pipette for the initial and final rinses when cleaning the worms free of bacteria, so that the very thin coating of bacteria left on the inside of the glass prevents worms from sticking to the pipette.

Slides can be stored before ‘cracking’ at -80°C for days to weeks before staining with some (but not all) antibodies. Let slides from -80°C ‘warm up’ on dry ice for 10 minutes before cracking.

If you do not have slabs of dry ice available, then you can use a pre-chilled conductive (metal) plate on top of crushed dry ice. You may also use a metal plate on the shelf of a -30°C or -80°C freezer. However, this later technique may not work as well as a slab of dry ice (and is not good for your freezer).

Some people use a coverslip as the top of the slide sandwich, instead of a commercial poly-L-lysine slide. This allows more delicate compression of the worms, but removal of the cover slip can be more difficult than separating the two slides. Removal of the cover slip should only be done when wearing goggles to protect your eyes from flying shards of glass.

Problems with recovering intact worms on the poly-lysine slides can arise from:

Insufficiently cleaned worms.
Insufficiently cleaned or poorly coated or poorly dried poly-lysine slides.
Too much or too little or sideways pressure during compression.
You can monitor worm compression by examining your slides with a dissection scope.
Harder fixation conditions lead to more loss of worms.
Air drying the slides may improve adhesion, but decreases staining with some antibodies.
Pouring solutions directly over the worms may wash them off of the slides.

You can make extra sticky poly-lysine slides (for hard fixes or precious worms) as follows:

Place a small drop of Lab-made poly-L-lysine Solution directly onto a clean slide. Either let the drop evaporate as is OR use a second slide to carefully spread the drop before drying. In either case, you should get a very sticky spot on your slide. Unfortunately, the poly-lysine spot will often bind non-specifically to your antibody and give high background staining.

2.7. Protocol 7: Tube fixation

2.7.1. General comments

This protocol can produce worms with fairly permeable cuticles and nice morphology (Li and Chalfie, 1990, Garriga et al., 1993). The related Peroxide tube fixation protocol uses additional reducing and oxidizing agents and omits the collagenase treatment (Finney and Ruvkun, 1990). In either case, the fixed and treated worms can be stored for months at -20°C.

Treatment with collagenase can be quite variable and the fixation and permeabilization conditions disrupt many antigens. However, the overall structure of the worm is often well preserved.

2.7.2. Protocols

Procedure 12. Fixation

Use 1-10 plates of healthy, well-fed worms.

  1. Wash the worms from the plates with M9 buffer and collect in a 1.5 ml or 15 ml tube.

  2. Spin the worms at 2000 rpm (~400 g) for 2-3 minutes.

    [To enrich for adults, let the worms settle for 5 minutes at room temperature.]

  3. Remove the supernatant and repeat M9 wash until ~ all bacteria are gone and solution is clear.

  4. Transfer worms to a new screw cap 1.5 ml tube, using dd water.

  5. Top tube with dd water.

  6. Spin 2 minutes at 2,000 rpm (~400 g).

  7. Remove all but 50-100 ul of solution.

  8. Put on ice for 2-3 minutes.

  9. Add 1 ml TOXIC Fix solution.

  10. Put in beaker containing dry ice + 95% ethanol for 2-3 minutes to freeze worms solid and crack their cuticles.

    [Optional: Use liquid nitrogen.]

  11. Defrost with warm water.

  12. Repeat the freeze and defrost steps 1-2 times (to crack as many worms as possible).

  13. Put on shaker at 4°C to 37°C for 1-24 hr.

  14. Spin at high speed, remove fixative (discard safely), and top with PBST.

  15. Repeat PBST rinse 3-5 times, until the fixative is no longer detectable.

    [Optional: Worms can be stored in PBST for up to one month at 4°C (or longer at -20°C).]

Reduction:

  1. Add 1 ml β-mercaptoethanol solution (βME).

  2. Shake at 100 rpm, 37°C for 2 hours (light fix) or up to 48 hours (hard fix).

  3. The worms should be whole, but are more fragile and sticky than before treatment.

  4. Spin at 5,000 rpm (~1,000 g) for 3 minutes, and then remove supernatant and top tube with PBST.

  5. Repeat spin and PBST rinses as many as 8 times, until the fixative is no longer detectable.

    [Optional: Worms can be stored in PBST for several days at 4°C (or longer at -20°C).]

Collagenase treatment

  1. As early in the day as possible: Place worms in clear 0.5 or 0.25 ml tube.

  2. Add 50-100 ul collagenase solution (1-2X volume of worms).

  3. Put on 37°C shaker at 250 rpm.

  4. Monitor the worms regularly (every 30 minutes) until many adults have broken in half.

  5. Check the status of the worms under the dissecting microscope by transferring a small aliquot to a clear slide or, with experience, by peering through the side of the tube under the microscope.

  6. The collagenase treatment can vary from 30 minutes to 36 hours depending upon the batch of collagenase, the fixation conditions, and the number of worms.

  7. When the worms are sufficiently broken, put the tube on ice for 2 minutes.

  8. Spin 2 minutes at 1,000 rpm (<400 g).

  9. If necessary (if the worms are stuck to the side of the tube), turn the tube 180 degrees, and re-spin.

  10. Remove the supernatant and top with PBST.

  11. Repeat the rinse 2 times with PBS.

    [Optional: Worms can be stored for several days at 4°C (or longer at -20°C).]

  12. Proceed with blocking and staining (see Staining tube-fixed worms protocol).

2.7.3. Comments

After the fixation and rinsing is complete (but before the βME treatment), worms can be stored in PBST for up to one month at 4°C (or longer at -20°C).

After the βME treatment (but before the collagenase treatment), worms can be stored in PBST for a few days at 4°C (or longer at -20°C).

After treatment with βME, the worms are sticky and fragile. Cut the tip off of the fine plastic pipette tip and rinse the tip with antibody solution before using it to transfer worms. If your worms are very precious, you can do all of the treatments in glass tubes, which are less sticky than plastic tubes.

2.9. Protocol 9: Peroxide tube fixation

2.9.1. General Comments

This fixation procedure uses formaldehyde as a fixative and is done on worms in tubes. It is similar to the Tube fixation and Bouin's tube fixation protocols, but uses relatively simple chemical treatments instead of collagenase to increase permeability of the cuticle (Finney and Ruvkun, 1990). This procedure is more reproducible and less expensive than the collagenase treatment, but does not work as well for some proteins.

You can prepare a stock of fixed and permeabilized worms and store them at 4°C or -20°C until needed.

2.9.2. Protocol

Procedure 15. Fixation

  1. Use 1-10 plates of healthy, well-fed worms.

  2. Wash the worms from the plates with M9 buffer and collect in a 1.5 ml or 15 ml tube.

  3. Spin the worms at 2000 rpm (~400 g) for 2-3 minutes.

    [Optional: To enrich for adults, let the worms settle for 5 minutes at room temperature.]

  4. Remove the supernatant and repeat M9 wash until ~ all bacteria are gone and solution is clear.

  5. Transfer worms to a new screw cap 1.5 ml tube, using dd water.

  6. Top tube with dd water.

  7. Spin 2 minutes at 2,000 rpm (~400 g).

  8. Remove all but 0.5 ml of solution.

  9. Put on ice for 2-3 minutes.

  10. Add 0.5 ml ice cold (TOXIC) Fixation Solution.

  11. Put tightly capped tube in a dry ice + 95% ethanol bath for 1 minute (to crack the cuticles).

    [Optional: Use liquid nitrogen.]

  12. Defrost tube with warm water.

    [Optional: Repeat freeze thaw up to three more times to crack more worms.]

  13. Shake for 10 minutes to overnight at 4°C.

    [Optional: Store frozen and fixed worms indefinitely at -20°C.]

Procedure 16. Permeabilization

  1. Spin at 10,000 rpm (>2,000 g) for 1 minute.

  2. Remove fix (discard safely) and change to Tris-Triton Solution (TT).

  3. Repeat spin and TT rinse.

  4. Remove TT and replace with Tris-Triton β-mercaptoethanol Solution (TTB).

  5. Shake in TTB at 37°C for 1-12 hours (longer incubations after longer fixations).

  6. Spin at 3,000 rpm (<1,000 g) for 2 minutes (low speed since the worms are more fragile).

  7. Transfer worms to a 15 ml screw cap tube containing 10 ml Borate buffer solution (BB).

  8. Spin at 3,000 rpm (<1,000 g) for 2 minutes and change to Borate buffer + DTT.

  9. Shake gently for 15 minutes at 37°C.

  10. Spin at 3,000 rpm (<1,000 g) for 2 minutes and change to Borate buffer.

  11. Spin at 3,000 rpm (<1,000 g) for 2 minutes and change to Borate buffer + peroxide.

  12. Shake gently for 15 minutes at room temperature.

    Note

    Pressure will develop in the tube - be sure to keep it upright in case the top comes off.

  13. Spin at 3,000 rpm (<1,000 g) for 2 minutes and change to Borate buffer.

  14. Spin at 3,000 rpm (<1,000 g) for 2 minutes and replace Borate buffer with Antibody buffer.

  15. Shake gently for 15 minutes at room temperature.

  16. Spin at 3,000 rpm (<1,000 g) for 2 minutes and move worms to 1.5 ml tube with Antibody buffer.

    [Optional: Store worms in Antibody buffer 1 month at 4°C or many months at -20°C.]

  17. Proceed with blocking and staining (see Staining tube-fixed worms protocol).

2.10. Protocol 10: Picric acid + glutaraldehyde fixation

2.10.1. General comments

This is a very “hard” fixation protocol that is used to crosslink the small diffusible neurotransmitters dopamine and octopamine (Monastirioti et al., 1995). Serotonin, GABA, and FMRFamide-related peptides do not require this fixative, but instead can be fixed with 4% formaldehyde or 4% formaldehyde/1% glutaraldehyde for 24 hours (Desai et al., 1988; Li and Chalfie, 1990; McIntire et al., 1992).

The fixation can be done on worms prepared on slides or in tubes.

The fixation procedure greatly increases autofluorescence of the cuticle and the entire worm, so it should only be used if absolutely required.

2.10.2. Protocols

With either protocol, wear gloves and a lab coat while working with fix and during the initial rinses.

Dispose of the used fix safely: Picric acid is toxic and explosive.

2.11. Protocol 11: Depletion of primary antibody

2.11.1. General comments

The following procedures are used for purifying antibodies when you have a null mutant that does not produce the protein of interest. In some cases, your affinity purified antibody may be specific enough that you do not need to do affinity depletion (see Affinity purification protocol). However, if the antibody shows non-specific staining, you may be able to improve its specificity with this technique.

Start with serum that has been affinity-depleted with bacteria and/or affinity-purified with peptide/protein and/or is untreated. The more the serum is pretreated, the more likely it is to work nicely after affinity-depletion with null worms. The simplest way to deplete serum with bacteria is by incubation in commercially available bacteria acetone powder (Harlow and Lane, 1988). If you used a fusion protein to generate your antibody, you can deplete your primary antibody with a protein preparation from the same bacterial system expressing an unrelated fusion protein.

The exact protocol to follow for cleaning primary antibodies with worms depends upon the volumes of serum and worms you have and the fixation technique you plan to use. Match the fixation technique you use for depleting the serum with the fixation conditions you plan to use to stain worms.

Start with incubating the (treated) serum with a volume of worms approximately equal to the original volume of (untreated) serum. The actual volume of treated serum that you use may be much greater than the volume of the worms, since most serum treatments lead to more dilute antibody. For example, if you started with 1 ml of serum and affinity purified it and ended up with 4 ml of solution, you would like to use 1 ml of worms.

Worm-depleted antibody is not as stable as non-cleaned antibody. Freeze or use the depleted antibody as soon as possible.

If the worm depletion works, then you can try depleting with fewer worms.

If your worms are hard to grow, you should be able to improve the specificity of your antibody, even if you can only clean a tiny volume of serum with an even smaller volume of worms.

2.11.2. Protocols

Procedure 19. For formaldehyde-fixed nulls

  1. Grow several plates of null worms.

    Try to use a volume of fixed worms equal to the original volume of the primary serum.

  2. Rinse worms off plates with water (food can be present) and collect in screw cap 1.5 ml tubes.

    Do not rinse more than necessary to collect the worms in the tubes.

  3. Spin the tube(s) of worms 5 minutes at high speed in a tabletop centrifuge (breaks some worms).

  4. Add 1 ml TOXIC Fix Solution (e.g., 1% Formaldehyde Solution).

  5. Put in dry ice + 95% ethanol bath for 2-3 minutes.

    [Optional: Use liquid nitrogen.]

  6. Defrost with warm water.

  7. Repeat the freeze and defrost steps 1-2 times (to crack as many worms as possible).

  8. Leave tube shaking vigorously at room temperature for one hour.

  9. Spin 2 minutes at high speed, remove fixative (discard safely), and top with PBS.

  10. Spin 2 minutes at high speed, and exchange PBS.

  11. Repeat rinse and spin.

  12. Shake 15 minutes at room temperature.

  13. Repeat rinse and spin 2 times.

    [Optional: Store extra worms at -20°C until needed.]

  14. Remove PBS and add serum.

  15. If necessary, dilute serum up to 1:10 with Antibody buffer so the worms are surrounded by the fluid.

  16. Incubate with vigorous shaking for 1-4 hours at room temperature.

  17. Spin 2 minutes at high speed and remove supernatant and save for staining wild-type worms.

  18. Use a small volume of antibody buffer to recover more of the serum: add to worms, shake, spin, remove supernatant, and add to previous supernatant.

Procedure 20. For methanol/acetone-fixed nulls

Follow above protocol except:

  1. Replace formaldehyde with a 1:1 mixture of methanol and acetone.

  2. Replace dry ice + ethanol bath with liquid N2 to freeze worms.

  3. Methanol/Acetone does not freeze in dry ice + 95 % ethanol.

  4. Incubate in fixation solution for only 15 minutes.

  5. Only 4 PBS rinses need to be done to rinse out the fixation solution.

Procedure 21. For nulls that are difficult to grow

Follow above protocol except:

  1. Pre-incubate the tube for collecting worms with Antibody buffer.

  2. Be sure all spins are long enough and fast enough to collect all worms.

  3. After freeze/thaws, use an Antibody buffer- soaked tiny pestle to grind up the worms.

    [Optional: Use a micro sonicator.]

  4. Use three rinses of Antibody buffer at the end to collect the worm depleted serum.

Procedure 22. For nulls that are extremely difficult to grow

  1. Do all of the incubation and rinses on individual slides.

  2. Generally follow the Freeze-crack protocol.

  3. Collect worms in a drop of water on a very sticky lab-made poly-lysine slide.

  4. Top with a commercial poly-lysine slide and place on dry ice.

  5. Prepare a flat humidified dish with lid.

  6. Crack slides and place bottom slide in the flat dish.

  7. Carefully place fix (1% formaldehyde or 1:1 methanol:acetone) on top of slide.

  8. Leave fix on for one hour (formaldehyde) or 5 minutes (methanol:acetone).

  9. Carefully draw off fix, trying to leave any worm pieces on slide.

  10. Rinse several times with PBS, trying to leave any worm pieces on slide.

  11. Rinse once with Antibody buffer.

  12. Make a well around the fixed worms with a grease pencil.

  13. Put diluted serum on slide for one hour (keep dish covered to prevent evaporation).

  14. Carefully remove serum with fine tipped pipette and save in a 1.5 ml tube.

  15. Use depleted antibody on a new slide on wild-type worms.

2.12. Protocol 12: Cleaning secondary antibody

2.12.1. General comments

Some secondary antibodies contain a significant level of anti-worm antibodies and should be cleaned (affinity depleted) for the highest staining specificity.

You can determine whether you need to clean your secondary antibody by incubating some fixed worms with 1:100 dilution of your secondary. If you see significant staining of your worms with the secondary alone, you can clean the secondary as described below.

Start with a volume of fixed worms approximately equal to the volume of the secondary.

2.12.2. Protocols

Incubation #1:

  1. Put 0.5-1.0 ml secondary into a new 1.5 ml screw cap tube.

  2. Add 0.5 volumes of ‘Fixed worms for cleaning’ and cap tightly.

  3. Put in light tight canister (e.g., empty film canister).

  4. Shake vigorously at 4°C for several hours to overnight.

  5. Spin tube 2 minutes at high speed in a table-top centrifuge.

  6. Transfer the secondary into a new tube.

  7. Add 0.2 ml PBS to the original tube with worms and spin again 2 minutes.

  8. Transfer any remaining worm-free liquid to the new tube of secondary.

  9. Add 0.2 ml PBS to the original tube with worms and spin again 2 minutes.

  10. Transfer any remaining worm-free liquid to the new tube of secondary.

  11. Discard used worms.

  12. Repeat spin and transfer as needed to remove any worms and recover all antibodies.

Incubation #2:

  • Repeat incubation #1 if you had a lot of staining with your secondary.

2.13. Protocol 13: Staining slides

2.13.1. General comments

These techniques are for staining slides generated with the Freeze-crack protocol (Strome and Wood, 1982; Albertson, 1984; Miller and Shakes, 1995; Duerr et al., 1999). See the Staining tube-fixed worms protocol for staining worms in 1.5 ml tubes.

GFP plus antibodies: GFP fluorescence is partially preserved by some fixation techniques. Generally, it withstands formaldehyde better than glutaraldehyde, acetone, or methanol. If you wish to examine antibody staining in a GFP+ strain, use a red secondary antibody (e.g., Cy3) to label your protein to avoid possible cross-talk with the GFP. If you want to examine antibody staining and GFP in the same worms, you can use one of the commercially available anti-GFP antibodies (Chemicon chicken anti-GFP or BD #8367 rabbit anti-GFP) and a green (e.g., Alexa 488 or Oregon Green 488) secondary to examining the distribution of your GFP. In some cases, anti-GFP antibodies can be more sensitive than GFP fluorescence.

2.13.2. Protocols

Procedure 23. Block non-specific protein binding

  1. Put the slides into a dish with Block for 1 hour at room temperature or overnight at 4°C.

  2. Filter and save Block (see notes).

  3. Proceed to Staining with large volumes of primary antibody or staining with small volumes of primary antibody.

Procedure 24. Staining with large volumes of primary antibody

Staining slides in jars of antibody gives the best staining.

  1. Pour Primary antibody solution into a coplin jar or a smaller staining vessel.

    [Optional: Use small plastic holders designed to hold two slides or five slides.]

  2. Transfer slides (back-to-back) from block into jar of primary.

  3. Incubate overnight at 4°C.

  4. Filter and save the primary antibody (see notes).

  5. Pour in PBS.

  6. Proceed to secondary antibody step.

Procedure 25. Staining with small volumes of primary antibody

Placing antibodies directly on slides uses much less antibody, but gives less uniform staining.

  1. Place slides in a very flat sealable ‘box.’ Two stacked 96-well plate lids work well as a ‘box’ to hold ~3 slides each.

  2. Humidify the ‘box’ with dd water (and damp towels, if necessary).

  3. Be sure the dish is flat on the counter in its final resting place.

  4. Remove a slide from the block.

  5. Completely dry the back and outer edges of the slide with a kimwipe.

  6. Draw a circle around the worms with a grease pencil (available at art stores).

  7. The surface that you draw on needs to be dry or the wax will not stick.

    Option: You may also use a PAP pen, but the grease pencil is much cheaper.

  8. Put the slide with the circle face up in the ‘dish.’

  9. Slides must not be in contact with water or with each other.

  10. Put on primary (20-100 ul per slide) as quickly as possible.

  11. Cover the ‘box’ with another 96-well plate lid.

  12. Leave overnight at room temperature on counter (do not move).

  13. If the antibody is especially precious, it can be recovered the next day using a micropipette.

  14. Antibodies have been reused up to 2 times on new slides.

  15. Rinse slides gently with PBS from a squirt bottle (do not squirt directly on worms).

  16. Put slides back-to-back in a new coplin jar of PBS.

  17. Proceed to secondary antibody step.

Procedure 26. Secondary antibody

  1. Pour out PBS from jar.

  2. Add antibody buffer to jar and leave at room temperature for 30 minutes.

    [Optional: Gentle rocking will increase specificity, but will shake lose some worms.]

  3. Exchange Antibody buffer and leave at room temperature for 30 minutes (optional shaking).

  4. Repeat Antibody buffer exchange and 30 minutes of incubation.

  5. Remove Antibody buffer.

  6. Add secondary antibody solution.

    Option: Use small plastic holders designed to hold two or five slides.

  7. Cover well to protect from light.

  8. Let sit 4 hours at room temperature (MAb or PAb) or overnight at 4°C (PAb).

  9. Pour off, filter, and save secondary antibody.

  10. Pour in, then immediately pour out, PBS.

  11. Add Antibody buffer to jar and leave at room temperature for 30 minutes (optional shaking).

  12. Exchange Antibody buffer and leave at room temperature for 30 minutes to overnight at 4°C.

  13. Overnight incubation may improve staining of high affinity antibodies, but may decrease staining with low affinity antibodies.

  14. Change solution to PBS.

  15. After at least 5 minutes, the slides are ready for ‘mounting.’

Procedure 27. Mounting slides

  1. Remove a slide from the PBS and place on a dry paper towel.

  2. Work quickly so that the top of the slide stays wet.

  3. Dry the back of the slide with a kimwipe.

  4. Place 2 small drops (~20 ul) of TOXIC mounting medium over worms.

  5. There should be approximately equal volumes mounting medium and PBS on the slide.

  6. Tilt slide to mix the mounting medium with the PBS.

  7. Gently put down edge of 24 × 60 mm coverslip on the slide starting near the frosted edge.

  8. Use fine forceps or your fingertips to hold and lower the cover slip.

  9. Slowly let coverslip down; try not to let any air bubbles form.

  10. If air bubbles form, lift the edge of the coverslip slowly, add more PBS and then lower the coverslip again without disturbing the worms.

  11. Carefully dry off the edge of the slide (without moving the coverslip).

  12. Seal the coverslip with two to three layers of nail polish.

  13. Store slides in a flat dark holder while nail polish is drying.

  14. After the polish is dry, if needed, carefully clean off the outer surfaces of the slide and coverslip.

  15. Keep the slides dark and cold to preserve staining.

  16. Antibody staining in sealed slides persists for days at 4°C or months at -20°C.

  17. DAPI staining of DNA fades and spreads after weeks at -20°C.

  18. Reseal the coverslip with more nail polish as needed, e.g., after using any oil immersion lens.

  19. Exposure to air will cause the fluorescence to fade.

Procedure 28. Antibody controls

The following work well on tissues fixed with a variety of different protocols:

Mouse anti-DNA (Chemicon Mab030 anti-DNA).
Mouse anti-tubulin (Amersham N357 anti-beta tubulin).
Mouse anti-actin (Calbiochem Ab-1 anti-actin; ICN Biochemicals C4 anti-actin).

  1. If your antibody was made in mouse, try rabbit polyclonal anti-actin antibodies.

  2. Test your fixation conditions and tissue permeability with known specific antibodies.

  3. Always test the specificity of your primary and secondary antibodies.

    Possible tests:

    Pre-absorb your primary antibody with your antigen.
    You can use the eluate from the Antibody purification protocol.
    Block specific binding by adding antigen to the primary antibody on slide during incubation.
    Examine null mutants or, if unavailable, partial loss of function mutants for decreased staining.
    Generate RNAi worms and examine staining.
    Examine transgenic over-expressing lines for increased staining.

2.13.3. Comments

If your worms were lightly fixed (e.g., methanol and acetone), you can do just two rinses of 20-30 minutes each after the primary and secondary antibodies incubations. Even shorter incubations and rinses may be attempted, but will decrease the signal to noise ratio for some antibodies.

If you need to use two antibodies that have both been generated in the same species, then double staining with both antibodies may be problematic. It is possible that the two antibodies will be different subtypes (e.g., IgG vs. IgM) and you can use subtype specific secondary antibodies. If not, you can easily directly label the primary antibodies with an activated fluorescent dye (available from Molecular Probes). Unfortunately, this removes one round of signal amplification, so only abundant proteins are likely to be detectable with directly labeled primaries.

You may stain filamentous actin with fluorescent phalloidin (Molecular Probes). After the secondary antibody and the rinses in Antibody buffer, but before the final PBS rinse, remove the slide from the jar and place in a humidified dish. Cover the worms with 100 ul PBS containing 1 unit of fluorescent-phalloidin and incubate 30 minutes in the dark. Place the slide in a coplin jar of PBS for 20 minutes in the dark before mounting. Be sure to pick a color of fluorescent phalloidin that is compatible with your secondary antibodies.

To see DNA with DAPI, you will need to illuminate the tissue with UV light. UV bleaches some fluorescent probes, especially green dyes such as fluorescein. Therefore, examine your green (and red) fluorescent staining before looking at the DAPI.

You may use propidium iodide (fluoresces red) to label DNA and RNA instead of DAPI (which fluoresces blue and binds DNA specifically).

For more comments on block, secondaries, and mounting medium, see below under the individual solutions.

Avoid repeated rounds of freezing and thawing of antibodies (or any other proteins). If you do refreeze a protein solution, be sure that it contains 1% or more protein.

2.15. Protocol 15: Worm protein preparation

2.15.1. General comments

This is a simple procedure to crack washed nematodes in a solubilizing and denaturing buffer (Francis and Waterston, 1985). For very abundant proteins, 10 minutes at 95°C in 1X protein dye (i.e., no special buffer) may be sufficient.

The need for each inhibitor in the protease inhibitor mix has not been carefully examined. The detection of some, but not all, antigens is enhanced in the presence of the inhibitor mix. You can test your individual protein to see if the inhibitor mix is necessary.

2.15.2. Protocols

Be sure all reagents and tools are on hand before starting.

Procedure 33. Sucrose wash worms

  1. Wash 10 plates with M9 and collect into a 15 or 50 ml conical tube.

  2. Spin 2 minutes at 1,000 rpm (~200 g).

  3. Change to fresh M9.

  4. Repeat spin and exchange 2 more times, ending with a spin.

  5. Place worms on ice 2 minutes.

  6. Remove M9.

  7. Add Sucrose solution.

  8. Immediately spin at 4°C at 1,000 rpm (~200 g) for 5 minutes.

  9. Immediately aspirate worms from top of fluid into new 15 ml conical tube.

  10. Immediately add water to dilute residual sucrose.

  11. Spin 1,000 rpm (~200 g) for 2 minutes.

  12. Retain pellet of intact worms in small amount of fluid.

  13. Use M9 to transfer worm ‘pellet’ to a screw cap 1.5 ml tube.

  14. Wash 3X with M9 (alternate 2 minute 2,000 rpm spins and exchange of M9).

  15. Remove fluid and leave worm pellet.

Procedure 34. Expose worm proteins

  1. Add a volume of Nematode solubilization buffer that is equal to the volume of the worm pellet.

  2. Immediately cap tightly and microwave 25 sec on high (for ~100 ul worms).

    Note

    Increase microwave time slightly as needed for larger volumes.

  3. Immediately add equal volume 2X protein dye + 2 ul 100X (extra) protease inhibitor.

  4. Immediately place in boiling water bath for 7 minutes.

  5. Quickly draw lysate into a syringe through a 26 gauge needle.

  6. Quickly force the lysate back through the needle into a new 1.5 ml tube (on ice).

  7. Spin 1 minute at high speed to remove insoluble components.

  8. Quickly transfer supernatant into a new tube (on ice).

  9. Immediately run on gel or store aliquots at -80°C.

2.15.3. Comments

NSB aliquots can be defrosted and refrozen two times (mark tubes before refreezing).

If your protein is robust, you may be able to omit the protease inhibitor.

If your protein is abundant and robust, you may be able to omit the NSB and protease inhibitor.

See Worm protein gel protocol for running your protein out on a mini-gel apparatus

2.17. Protocol 17: Western transfer

2.17.1. General comments

For general information on Western Transfer protocols, see Harlow and Lane (1988) and Harlow (1999). These protocols are for transfer with two popular brands; the Biorad Mini-Protean II Trans-Blot (wet) Transfer Cell and the Owl Semi-Dry Apparatus.

Most (worm) proteins are relatively low in abundance. This is especially true if you are examining a tissue- specific protein, but are using protein from the entire worm.

Methods to increase sensitivity:

Use a membrane with high binding capability. Nitrocellulose generally has higher binding than PVDF and different brands of nitrocellulose have different binding abilities.
A traditional ‘wet’ transfer apparatus may work better than a ‘semi-dry’ transfer apparatus.
Transfer slowly at a relatively low current, e.g., overnight at 4°C.
Use long incubation times in the antibody solutions to maximize staining.
Use a sensitive enzymatic substrate or a chemiluminescent substrate.

Mouse anti-tubulin (e.g., Amersham N357 mouse monoclonal anti-beta tubulin) or mouse anti-actin (e.g., Calbiochem Ab-1 mouse monoclonal anti-actin) may be used as a positive control for protein loading and transfer.

Primary and secondary antibodies may bind non-specifically to many worm proteins. If you have a null mutant, you should make a Western Blot using protein from the mutant and compare staining with that seen with wild-type worms. You can also use the membrane from the null worm to affinity deplete your primary antibody (see Affinity purification protocol). If no null mutant is available, a partial loss-of-function mutant, RNAi strain or a (transgenic) over-expressing strain may be useful in determining which bands are specific for the protein of interest.

Membranes containing Western blots of worms may be used to pre-absorb the secondary antibody.

See the Staining slides protocol for more discussion of using antibodies to stain worm proteins, including primaries, secondaries, and controls.

2.17.2. Protocols

Procedure 36. Transfer in Biorad Mini-Protean II Apparatus

  1. Wear gloves (to protect the membrane from you and to protect you from the methanol).

  2. Chill 1 L Transfer buffer to 4°C.

  3. Fill a 600 ml beaker with ~100 ml dd water.

  4. Gently take out 1-2 pieces of high quality nitrocellulose, holding only the edges with forceps.

  5. Never touch the center of the membrane, this will cause blotchiness.

  6. Cut the membrane slightly larger than the gel and mark a corner with pencil.

  7. Set the nitrocellulose on edge in the beaker of water and let the water wick up the membrane.

  8. Collect the following:

    2 pieces of Whatman #3 filter paper about 3"X4" per gel.
    2 coarse white spongy filters per gel.
    9"X13" or similar size glass or plastic pan.
    Conical tube or pipette to use as rolling pin.
    Transfer holder, tank, lid, and black and white cassettes.

  9. Pour about half of the cold transfer buffer into the large pan.

  10. Presoak the Whatman filters and white spongy filters in the large pan.

  11. Open one cassette and place the black side down on the bottom of the pan.

  12. Place a spongy filter on the black side of the cassette.

  13. Roll the pipette repeatedly over the filter to remove any air bubbles (air bubbles prevent transfer).

  14. Place a soaked Whatman filter over the spongy filter and roll to remove air bubbles.

  15. Remove the gel from the running apparatus so that it is lying on one of the glass plates.

  16. Holding the glass plate, place the gel down evenly on the Whatman filter.

  17. Peel back the corners of the filter so that the gel adheres to the filter.

  18. The gel should be placed on the filter close to the fold in the cassette (deeper in the tank).

  19. Slowly remove the glass.

  20. Drop a few drops of transfer buffer onto the gel.

  21. Roll the gel to make it smooth and remove air; do not roll hard enough to deform the gel.

  22. Pick up the totally wet nitrocellulose with forceps and place it in the transfer buffer.

  23. The nitrocellulose must be totally wet; if the top is dry, rotate and keep in water until wet.

  24. Gently place the membrane on the gel with the pencil mark on the upper left corner of the gel.

  25. Roll gently to remove air bubbles, do not deform gel.

  26. Top the membrane with a soaked Whatman filter.

  27. Roll to remove air bubbles.

  28. Top with a soaked spongy filter.

  29. Roll to remove air bubbles.

  30. Close and ‘lock’ the cassette.

  31. Place the cassette in its holder so that the black side is facing the black side of the holder.

  32. Repeat with a second gel, if desired.

  33. Place the holder in the tank and pour in transfer buffer to the top of the middle region of the holder (this is less than 1 L). Solution will pour out the sides if it is too full.

  34. Put on lid (matching red leads) and plug into a small power supply.

    Be sure your power supply can provide the necessary current.

  35. Turn on the power supply and set it for constant current.

  36. Start the transfer: you should see fine bubbles coming up from the black side of the holder.

  37. Run at 50 mAmp overnight in the cold room.

  38. Proceed to Staining membrane step.

Procedure 37. Fixation transfer in Owl© semi-dry apparatus

  1. Wear gloves (to protect the membrane from you and to protect you from the methanol).

  2. Chill 200 ml Transfer Buffer to 4°C.

  3. Fill a 600 ml beaker with ~100 ml dd water.

  4. Gently take out 1-2 pieces of high quality nitrocellulose, holding only the edges with forceps.

  5. Never touch the center of the membrane, this will cause blotchiness.

  6. Cut the membrane slightly larger than the gel and mark a corner with pencil.

  7. Set the nitrocellulose on edge in the beaker of water and let the water wick up the membrane.

  8. Cut 6 pieces of filter (blotting) paper so that they are the same size as the nitrocellulose.

  9. Presoak the filter paper in a shallow dish of transfer buffer.

  10. Fill the lower ‘chamber’ of the transfer apparatus with transfer buffer.

  11. Stack 3 pieces of the presoaked filter paper.

  12. Using the glass plate and a few ml of transfer buffer, transfer the gel to the top of the filter stack.

  13. Use a glass pipette to roll out any air bubbles (air bubbles prevent transfer).

  14. Using forceps, dip the hydrated nitrocellulose into the shallow dish of transfer buffer.

  15. Carefully place the nitrocellulose over the gel stack, using buffer and a pipette as needed to align the gel and nitrocellulose and filter paper.

  16. Place the mark on the membrane so it is over the upper left corner of the gel.

  17. Carefully add 3 pieces of presoaked filter paper, maintaining the alignment of the sandwich.

  18. Hold the stacks in place while you drain excess buffer from the plate.

  19. Wipe away any droplets around the edge of the stack.

  20. Place on the lid and loosely screw down to hold in place (do NOT tighten screws).

  21. Attach the power leads (red to red and black to black).

  22. Put on lid (matching red leads) and plug into a small power supply.

    Be sure your power supply can provide the necessary current.

  23. Turn on the power supply and set it for constant current.

  24. Run overnight at 4°C, using ~2 mAmp of current per square cm of membrane.

  25. Proceed to Staining membrane step

Procedure 38. Staining membrane

  1. After transfer, put on gloves, unplug apparatus, remove ‘sandwiches’ and discard buffer.

  2. Carefully remove the membrane from the sandwich with forceps.

  3. If the transfer has worked properly, you should see the markers on the membrane.

  4. Save the gel and fix and stain it to check for complete transfer (see Worm protein gel protocol).

  5. Put membrane (marked side up) in flat bottomed dish with PBS, no azide.

  6. Rinse the membrane 5 times with PBS, no azide.

  7. Cover membrane with membrane block.

  8. Shake for 1-2 hours at room temperature.

  9. Rinse membrane 2 X 5 minutes in PBS on shaker.

  10. Cover membrane with Primary antibody solution.

  11. Leave membrane on shaker at 4°C overnight.

  12. Rinse membrane 4 X 5 minutes in PBS (no azide).

  13. Cover membrane with HRP-conjugated Secondary antibody solution.

  14. Shake for 1-2 hours at room temperature.

  15. Pour a small amount of TMB substrate into a small flat covered dish to warm to room temperature.

    Option: Warm up a chemiluminescent substrate.

  16. Fill a 600 ml beaker with dd water.

  17. Rinse membrane 4 X 5 minutes in PBS (no azide).

  18. With forceps, put the membrane into the dish with TMB and shake well to cover.

    Option: Use the chemiluminescent substrate, following the manufacturer's directions.

  19. Let react for 1-15 minutes, checking for nice dark bands (dry membranes are fainter).

  20. To stop the reaction, transfer the membrane with tweezers to the beaker of dd water.

  21. Leave in dd water > 1 minute.

  22. Place wet membrane on a clean glass plate and take a photo.

  23. Air dry the membrane, protected from bright light.

  24. Seal the membrane in plastic and store in the dark.

2.17.3. Comments

You will need a lot of pre-stained marker to see it well after transfer (5 ul per well).

For abundant proteins, PVDF membranes may be used instead of nitrocellulose. PVDF is easier to use, but it holds less protein. PVDF should be presoaked briefly in methanol, not water.

You can vary the percentage of methanol in the transfer buffer: lower percentages help large proteins transfer out of the gel; higher percentages help small proteins stick to the membrane.

Membranes after transfer and before primary can be stored at -20°C in membrane block in a sealed container protected from mechanical disturbance for up to 2 months.

To increase sensitivity, react with a chemiluminescent substrate instead of TMB after incubation in secondary, following manufacturer's directions.

Membrane discolorations after TMB:

If you touched the membrane or stressed it while cutting or moving it, you may see dark blobs.
If the membrane was not totally within the transfer buffer, then the edge may be dark blue.
If the transfer gets too hot, faint circles may appear on the membrane.
If there are air bubbles, sharp white areas will appear on the membrane after TMB.

Worm Westerns often have nonspecific bands from the primary or secondary antibodies.

To check for nonspecific primary binding, try to compare binding in normal versus mutant strains.

To check for nonspecific secondary binding, try a no-primary control. To eliminate nonspecific secondary bands, you can pre-incubate diluted secondary antibody with a membrane that has worm protein on it.
